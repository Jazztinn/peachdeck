# Investor Quality Bar

Use this reference before finalizing deck narrative, claims, and fundraising logic. The deck should feel like a sharp founder explaining a real business, not like a template was filled in.

## First-Minute Test

Within the first minute, the audience should know:

- What the company does.
- Who it serves.
- Why the problem matters now.
- What proof exists.
- Why this team has a right to win.

If any answer is unclear, fix the opening before polishing design.

## Seibel Seed-Pitch Rules

Use these rules for pre-product-market-fit fundraising decks:

- Stand out by being concise and easy to understand, not by adding sizzle.
- Explain what the company does in two sentences, then give a specific example.
- Use one investor-facing explanation and a separate customer-facing explanation; website copy is often wrong for investors.
- Prefer a clear, simplified explanation over a technically complete but confusing one.
- Do not leave the first slide until the investor understands what the company does.
- Put "what we do" first, put the ask last, and order the middle by what is most impressive.
- Treat the pitch as a conversation, not a book report.
- Keep slides visually simple so attention stays on the founder and the argument.

## Seed-Stage Filters

Seed investors often filter hard on team, traction, and market size. Treat those as proof slides, not administrative slides.

- Team: show founder-market fit, rare technical ability, domain access, customer access, or execution speed.
- Traction: show evidence that customers care or that the team moves fast. Use real metrics with time periods, denominators, and elapsed time.
- Unique insight: show a non-obvious truth about the problem, customer, or solution, backed by a story, number, or fact.
- Market size: show a credible path from beachhead to large outcome. Use bottom-up math by default.

## Evidence Hierarchy

Prefer stronger evidence over louder claims:

1. Revenue, retention, repeat usage, signed contracts.
2. Paid pilots, credible pipeline, usage growth, activation, conversion.
3. Customer quotes from named or well-described users.
4. Prototype demos, benchmark results, waitlists with qualification.
5. Founder insight, market research, or analogies.

If evidence is weak, state what has been proven and what remains a risk.

## Narrative Standards

- Lead with a plain-language description of the company.
- Turn each slide into a claim the deck proves.
- Use customer language, not internal product language.
- Put the strongest proof before or immediately after the product explanation.
- Show the wedge: the narrow first market where the product can win.
- Show the expansion path only after the wedge is credible.
- Make the ask tied to a milestone, not just a budget.
- Make the specific example do work: it should reveal the customer, pain, product, and outcome without requiring a separate abstract explanation.

## Copy Standards

- Slide titles should be 4-10 words when possible.
- Body copy should usually fit in 2-4 bullets or one short paragraph.
- Every number needs units, time period, and source or basis.
- Replace adjectives with evidence: "fast" becomes "cuts intake from 18 minutes to 4."
- Avoid vague words: platform, seamless, revolutionary, leverage, unlock, next-gen, ecosystem, AI-powered unless the claim is made concrete.

## Risk Handling

Do not hide weak spots. Investors and judges notice missing logic fast.

- If there is no traction, omit the traction slide or use validation and learning velocity only when it is real.
- If market size is uncertain, show bottom-up assumptions and mark them.
- If competition is strong, explain the wedge, not why incumbents are bad.
- If the product is early, show demo proof and the next milestone.
- If the team lacks a role, state the hiring or advisor plan.

## Common Failure Modes

- Opening with a slogan instead of what the company does.
- Building a beautiful deck around a vague business.
- Claiming a giant market without a reachable first segment.
- Hiding the product until late in the deck.
- Treating the team slide as resumes instead of risk reduction.
- Listing fake traction such as superficial advisor names, generic surveys, or activity without company progress.
- Showing a flat or noisy graph just because the template expects a chart.
- Listing features without showing the user outcome.
- Ending without a specific ask.
