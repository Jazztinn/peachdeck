# Deck Structure

Use the shortest deck that gives the audience enough confidence to take the next action. Start by making the company easy to understand, end with the ask, and order the middle slides by what is most impressive.

## Choose A Spine

### Seed Or Pre-Seed Fundraising

Use 10-12 core slides plus appendix. Keep "What We Do" first and "Ask" last; move Team, Traction, or Unique Insight earlier when one of them is the strongest proof.

1. Cover: company name, one-line description, presenter, contact.
2. What We Do: two-sentence explanation plus a specific example that shows the problem and solution.
3. Strongest Proof: traction, team, or insight, whichever best earns the next two minutes.
4. Problem/Customer: painful, frequent, expensive, or urgent customer pain with precise ICP.
5. Solution/Product: how the product works, ideally with screenshot, workflow, or demo path.
6. Traction/Proof: revenue, usage, pilots, retention, waitlist quality, LOIs, shipping speed, or strong qualitative evidence.
7. Unique Insight: what the team knows about the problem, customer, or solution that is non-obvious.
8. Market Size: credible bottom-up market math tied to the beachhead and expansion path.
9. Business Model/GTM: pricing, distribution, sales motion, and early repeatability.
10. Competition/Positioning: why customers choose this over alternatives or doing nothing.
11. Team: why this team is unusually likely to win, if not already used as strongest proof.
12. Ask/Use Of Funds: amount, milestone, timeline, and why the raise creates step-change value.
13. Appendix: data, product depth, financials, technical architecture, customer quotes.

### Hackathon Or Demo-Day Deck

Use 6-8 slides:

1. Cover: project name, one-line outcome, team.
2. Problem: who hurts and what is broken today.
3. Solution/Demo: what you built and the primary user flow.
4. Technical Approach: architecture, model/API choices, data, and what is novel.
5. Impact/Market: why the project matters beyond the demo.
6. Validation: user feedback, benchmark, live demo result, or measurable improvement.
7. Team: relevant skills and division of work.
8. Ask/Next Step: prize category, pilot, open source plan, user signup, or roadmap.

### Sales Or Partnership Pitch

Use 7-10 slides:

1. Customer problem and business impact.
2. Product fit for that customer's workflow.
3. Proof from similar users or credible prototype evidence.
4. Implementation path and risk reduction.
5. Economics, ROI, or operational benefit.
6. Security, compliance, or integration details if needed.
7. Proposed pilot, success metrics, timeline, and decision ask.

### Review Or Rewrite

When reviewing a deck, preserve the user's intent but recommend a cleaner order. Show a "current issue" and "recommended replacement" for the most important slides before polishing copy.

## Slide Recipes

### Cover

- Title: company name or project name.
- Subtitle: one-line company description.
- Visual: product screenshot, customer workflow, or simple brand-forward composition.
- Avoid: slogans that do not say what the company does.

### What We Do

- State the company in two sentences or fewer.
- Name the customer, product, business model if relevant, and outcome.
- Add one specific example with a concrete person, situation, pain, and result.
- Use plain nouns and verbs; no category buzzwords unless the market already uses them.
- Optimize for investor understanding, not website positioning.

### Problem

- Show a specific customer, workflow, and pain.
- Make the cost visible: time, money, error rate, churn, risk, or missed revenue.
- Avoid vague claims like "the process is inefficient" unless paired with a concrete example.

### Solution/Product

- Explain the product through the user's path.
- Use screenshots, sequence diagrams, short demo steps, or before/after comparison.
- Connect features to outcomes instead of listing capabilities.

### Traction/Proof

- Rank evidence by strength: revenue, retention, active usage, signed customers, paid pilots, repeatable pipeline, strong LOIs, waitlist with qualification, user interviews.
- Include denominator and time period for metrics.
- Include elapsed time since starting or since the relevant milestone so momentum is visible.
- For pre-launch companies, show shipping speed, prototype progress, users in test, learning velocity, or other real progress.
- Use a graph only when it clearly moves up and to the right.
- If traction is early, state the learning signal honestly and pair it with next milestone.
- If traction is nonexistent, omit the slide instead of filling it with fake work.

### Unique Insight

- State a non-obvious thing learned about the problem, customer, or solution.
- Explain how the team learned it.
- Support it with numbers, user evidence, or a concrete story.
- Skip or move to appendix if the insight is obvious or generic.

### Market Size

- Prefer bottom-up math: number of reachable customers x realistic annual contract value.
- Show beachhead first, then expansion market.
- Explain pricing through comparable products, customer value, or current spend.
- Avoid unsupported "1% of a huge market" math.

### Business Model/GTM

- Name the buyer, sales motion, pricing basis, acquisition channel, and expected sales cycle.
- For hackathons, replace with adoption plan if there is no business model yet.

### Competition

- Include direct competitors, manual workarounds, incumbent tools, and "do nothing."
- Position on customer outcomes, speed, cost, integration, data advantage, or distribution.
- Do not claim "no competition."

### Team

- Tie each founder or member to execution risk: domain access, technical ability, distribution, prior shipping, customer insight.
- For hackathons, include concrete contributions rather than job titles only.

### Ask

- Make the action clear.
- Fundraising ask: amount, runway, milestones, current committed capital or social proof if relevant, and use of funds.
- Tie use of funds to revenue, usage, product, or market milestones rather than a hiring list.
- Hackathon ask: prize, users, pilot partner, grant, or next build milestone.
- Sales ask: pilot scope, decision maker, success metric, and date.
