## Scope

Apply this guidance mainly to pre-product-market-fit and seed-stage fundraising. The talk explicitly distinguishes this from later Series A fundraising.

## Core Thesis

Founders stand out by being concise and easy to understand. The deck should not try to sound fancy, cinematic, or overly designed. If an investor cannot quickly understand what the company does, the founder has created the main barrier to funding.

## Required Pitch Elements

Be able to cover:

1. What the company does.
2. Who is on the team.
3. What traction or momentum exists.
4. What unique insight the team has.
5. How large the market can become.
6. What the founder is asking for.

## What The Company Does

- Explain the company in two sentences.
- Add a specific, concrete example immediately after the explanation.
- Use investor language, not customer website copy. Investors usually do not share the customer's jargon or context.
- Prefer being roughly right and totally clear over technically complete and confusing.
- Make the example vivid enough to show both problem and solution.
- Do not leave the first slide before the investor knows what the company does.
- If there is any doubt, prompt the presenter to ask whether the investor understands or wants another example.

## Team

- State who is on the team, each person's role, and who writes code or builds the product.
- Identify the CEO clearly when fundraising.
- Include specific impressive accomplishments, not a life story.
- If possible, connect the team to lived experience with the problem.
- Treat the team slide as risk reduction: why this group is credible for this problem.

## Traction

- Define traction as what the team has accomplished since starting and why that progress is impressive.
- Include the time period. Momentum only makes sense when the audience knows whether the work took weeks, months, or years.
- Pre-launch traction can count if it shows speed, shipping, users, learning, or product progress.
- Use a graph only when it is genuinely up and to the right.
- Do not pad with fake work such as shallow surveys, advisor lists, or activity that does not move the company forward.
- If traction is weak or nonexistent, omit the traction slide and lean into team, insight, or speed of execution.

## Unique Insights

- Include non-obvious things learned about the problem, customer, or solution.
- Earn the right to present insights by first making the company and team understandable.
- Test whether the insight is actually unique: if most people would agree immediately, it is not a differentiated insight.
- Use specific stories, numbers, and facts to make insights credible.

## Market Size

- The method matters more than the final big number.
- Use bottom-up math: reachable users or customers x plausible price.
- Explain why the price is plausible by referencing comparable products or value created.
- Avoid quoting broad market reports as the main proof.
- Teach the investor how the market works; this makes the founder look like an expert.

## Ask

- Explicitly ask for money or the desired next action.
- State how much is being raised, social proof if relevant, and the milestone the money enables.
- Focus use of funds on revenue, usage, product, or milestone outcomes rather than a hiring list.
- For angels, asking matters because some investors are persuadable once saying no becomes uncomfortable.

## Overall Delivery

- Put "what we do" first and the ask last.
- Order the middle slides by strength, not by a rigid template. If traction is the strongest proof, show it early. If team is strongest, show team early.
- Earn the next two minutes of attention with each section; do not bury the best proof late.
- Do not deliver the pitch like a book report. Use the deck to start a conversation.
- Watch the investor's face and attention. When they show interest, follow that thread, even if it means jumping ahead.
- Let the investor talk. The more they verbalize their own reasoning, the more they can convince themselves.
- Keep slides visually simple. The investor should focus on the founder and the argument, not decorative slide design.

## Peachdeck Implications

- Generate seed decks with a clear two-sentence "what we do" slide plus a concrete example.
- Build traction slides around momentum and elapsed time, not just absolute numbers.
- Include a unique insight slide when the insight is specific and non-obvious.
- Make market slides bottom-up by default.
- Make the ask slide explicit and milestone-based.
- Prefer simple, clear slide design over visual novelty for fundraising decks.
